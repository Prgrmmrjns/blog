## Why I love reading

Reading is an activity that requires focus, patience and commitment. Skills that people nowadays often do not have. Reading for me is a kind of exercise in staying focused. On the other hand reading sometimes can be very inspirational. With movies you don’t have to visualize the story and the characters - it is done for you. 

However, with books you have to imagine for yourself how the main protagonist looks like, what the environment is like and so on. Reading requires and trains your imagination. And as another benefit reading may also help you to learn another language in a fun way! This way I read tons of books in English, French, Dutch etc. also with the purpose to improve my language skills. 

So to get you hooked on reading or to inspire you for your next read I will present now my Top 10 favourite books. Note that the order in which I list them is arbitrary - also simply because you cannot really compare books of genres like productivity and sci-fi. They serve very different purposes. 

## 1. The A Game of Ice and Fire series by G.R.R. Martin

There are probably only few other book series that can be compared with the depth of the world of G.R.R. Martin. I read all the books - in German there are 10 of them - and each one is more tense than the other. The plots, detailed descriptions of places and people, the intense writing all contributes to an amazing unforgettable immersive experience when reading this book. Most people probably will know A Game of Ice and Fire by the Game of Thrones TV series. I also loved these series, well at least until season 5-6, the point at which the TV writers had no clue how the book plot continues. This alone should give you a clue at how good the books are.

One of the most impressive aspects of the series is Martin's ability to create morally complex characters who defy simple categorization as heroes or villains. Characters like Jaime Lannister, who begins as a seemingly one-dimensional villain, evolve into nuanced individuals with understandable motivations. The unpredictable nature of the story, where beloved characters can meet sudden deaths and political fortunes can reverse in an instant, creates a sense of tension unlike any other fantasy series I've read.

## 2. The Foundation trilogy by Isaac Asimov

Science Fiction is by far my favourite book genre. With Sci-Fi authors map their visions or pose warnings of current trends projected into the future. How does the technology and society look like in 100, 1000 or millions of years from now? What happens if humanity comes into contact with aliens? And what places do we get to visit in our galaxy and perhaps even beyond that? Isaac Asimov is one of the pioneers of the Sci-Fi genre and his Foundation trilogy showed the impressive imagination of the author.  

The trilogy captivates with its grand vision of a galactic empire spanning thousands of worlds that is on the brink of collapse. Asimov's protagonist Hari Seldon develops "psychohistory," a mathematical science that can predict the future behavior of large populations. What makes this story so fascinating is how it combines elements of sociology, history, and mathematics to explore the rise and fall of civilizations.

Unlike many sci-fi works that focus on technology, Foundation's strength lies in its exploration of human nature, politics, and the inevitable cycles of history. Asimov masterfully creates a universe where individual actions seem insignificant against the backdrop of historical forces, yet pivotal characters still manage to change the course of history. The books make you think about determinism versus free will, and whether humanity's future is indeed predictable or not.

## 3. Dune - The Desert planet by Frank Herbert

Dune by Frank Herbert is a simple masterpiece of imagination. Unlike other Sci-Fi worlds, in Dune computers and AI don’t play a role after they rebelled againt humanity in the past. There are also no aliens in the world, body shields render firing guns useless and thus fights are done with mostly swords. Also the politics resembles medieval politics with few major Houses such as the Atreides or Harkonnens, thousands of minor houses and an emperor. 

What makes Dune truly exceptional is its intricate world-building. The desert planet Arrakis, with its harsh environment and giant sandworms, feels incredibly real and tangible. I was fascinated by Herbert's detailed portrayal of the Fremen culture, their water conservation practices, and their symbiotic relationship with the desert. The spice melange, which extends life and grants prescient abilities while being the most valuable substance in the universe, creates a perfect catalyst for the political and economic conflicts that drive the story. The ecological themes woven throughout the narrative were revolutionary for science fiction of that era and remain relevant today.

I particularly loved the character of Paul Atreides and his complex hero's journey. His transformation from privileged heir to messianic figure among the Fremen raises fascinating questions about destiny, free will, and the ethics of war. The political intrigue between the great Houses, the Emperor, and the Spacing Guild creates layers of tension that kept me engrossed throughout the book. Herbert doesn't shy away from exploring the darker consequences of Paul's actions, making the story morally nuanced in a way that many science fiction works aren't. This combination of philosophical depth, political machinations, and vivid imagination makes Dune not just entertaining, but genuinely thought-provoking.

## 4. The Last Orc by Silvana de Mari

Ok this is an odd one that many of you probably don’t know. I read the three books of the The last Elven series by Silvana de Mari when I was around 12 or 13 and I was completely sucked into this world of elves and orcs (but no hobbits). What made these books special to me as a young reader was the beautiful way they handled themes of prejudice, friendship across differences, and finding one's place in a hostile world. The characters are vividly drawn and emotionally complex, particularly the orc whose journey from outcast to hero resonated deeply with me during my teenage years.

Despite my fondness for these books, I find myself in a difficult position as a reader. The story itself is compelling - it follows the journey of the last orc named Rankstrail who, together with a young elf, embarks on a quest to find a hidden prophecy. The worldbuilding is rich and immersive, with a unique take on fantasy creatures that differs significantly from the Tolkien archetypes most of us are familiar with.

However, I now find myself having to separate the art from the artist. Recently I found out that Silvana de Mari has become infamous for her homophobic, racist and anti-science comments. While the books themselves promote acceptance and understanding, the author's public statements stand in stark contrast to these values. This creates a genuine dilemma: can I recommend books that touched me deeply when I cannot endorse the author's harmful views? I include this series because of its genuine impact on me as a young reader, but I do so with this important caveat.

## 5. The Harry Potter books by J.K. Rowling

One of the fondest memories I have from my childhood is me, my and my brother listening to my parents reading is the Harry Potter books before bed. We would crawl under the blanket and imagine we were wizards 🧙🏽‍♂️ and would create the most delicious food, run at lightning speed or fight evil people. Later when I could read my first books were obviously the Harry Potter books and I would read the Harry Potter books for hours until late at night. What captivated me most in the Harry Potter books is the deep friendship of Harry with Ron and Hermione and how you could achieve anything with just holding together as a team. 

I particularly appreciate how the wizarding world serves as a mirror to our own, addressing issues of prejudice (pure-bloods versus muggle-borns), corrupt governance (the Ministry of Magic's denial and later complicity), and the moral ambiguity of characters like Severus Snape and Albus Dumbledore. 

The worldbuilding is remarkably detailed and consistent, creating a universe that feels like it could exist alongside our own. From the intricate rules of Quidditch to the complex historical background of Hogwarts and its founders, Rowling crafted a world that readers can easily immerse themselves in. This attention to detail is why so many fans (myself included) could debate minor plot points and character motivations for hours.

While I acknowledge that recent controversies surrounding J.K. Rowling with her transphobic position have complicated many readers' relationship with the series, I still value the lessons of courage, friendship, and standing up for what's right that these books instilled in me during my formative years.

## 6. Atomic Habits by James Clear

Atomic Habits has completely changed my life. It's the book I keep returning to whenever I want to boost my productivity or develop new habits. James Clear has an incredible ability to translate complex behavioral science concepts into practical, actionable strategies. What makes this book special is not only the scientific background but also the humorous way Clear conveys his points – I often found myself smiling while simultaneously gaining important insights.

The central idea of the book – that small, continuous improvements of just 1% daily lead to massive results over time – has fundamentally changed my mindset. Instead of focusing on large, overwhelming goals, I've learned to concentrate on daily systems and processes. This has helped me implement sustainable changes in my life, from regular exercise to better time management.

What I particularly liked is Clear's four-step model for habit formation: make it obvious, make it attractive, make it easy, and make it satisfying. This simple but effective formula has helped me break many of my bad habits and promote positive ones.

For anyone reading this book, here are some tips I found particularly helpful:

- **Apply the 1% rule:** Aim to get just 1% better every day. These small improvements compound over time to enormous results.
- **Use habit stacking:** Connect a new habit with an already established one. Example: "After brushing my teeth, I will meditate for 10 minutes."
- **Design your environment:** Set up your environment so that good habits are obvious and easy, while bad habits become invisible and difficult.
- **Build in rewards:** Make sure your new habits are immediately rewarding to increase the likelihood you'll stick with them.

Without exaggeration, I can say that Atomic Habits deserves its place as one of the most successful productivity books of all time. It's not just a book but a toolkit for a better life – and in my case, it has done exactly that.

## 7. The Three Body Problem (Series) by Cixin Liu

The "Three-Body Problem" trilogy by Cixin Liu has profoundly impacted me and fundamentally changed my understanding of science fiction. What makes this series so special is the author's bold vision that combines scientific precision with philosophical depth.

What fascinated me most is Liu's unique portrayal of an alien civilization. The Trisolarans, who live in a solar system with three suns, have developed a completely different evolution and psychology than humans. Their unstable home system with unpredictable "stable epochs" and "chaotic epochs" has produced a species that values transparency above all and knows no individual privacy. This fundamental otherness makes the Trisolarans one of the most fascinating alien depictions in science fiction.

The three-body problem itself—a classic physics problem about predicting the movement of three celestial bodies—serves as a brilliant metaphor for the unpredictability of the universe and our future. The detailed description of the Trisolaris system sparked my interest in astronomy and physics and led me to learn more about celestial mechanics and chaotic systems.

Liu masterfully explores the psychology of survival in a potentially hostile galaxy. The "dark forest" theory—the idea that all intelligent life forms in the universe must remain silent and hidden to survive—is a frighteningly plausible answer to the Fermi paradox. This theory has fundamentally changed my understanding of why, despite the statistical probability of intelligent life, we haven't yet discovered aliens.

Particularly impressive is how Liu connects scientific concepts with human psychology. The "Wallfacer" strategy, where selected individuals must develop secret plans that remain hidden even from advanced surveillance technology, demonstrates a deep understanding of strategic thinking and human creativity.

> **"In this world, there are things that can only be protected by silence; there are people who can only survive by being forgotten; there are truths that can never be revealed."** - Cixin Liu, "The Dark Forest"
> 

The Three-Body Problem trilogy is not just an exciting story about interstellar conflict but also a profound meditation on the nature of civilization, the challenges of communication between fundamentally different species, and the possible future of humanity on a cosmic scale. It's a work that has made me look at our place in the universe in an entirely new way.

## 8. Sapiens by Yuval Noah Harari

*Sapiens* by Yuval Noah Harari is one of those rare books that completely reframes how you see humanity and our place in the world. What makes this book so remarkable is Harari’s ability to compress the entire history of humankind into a narrative that is both intellectually rigorous and deeply thought-provoking.

What fascinated me most were the philosophical metaphors Harari uses to make complex ideas accessible and memorable. He doesn’t just list historical events — he explores how shared myths, religions, and ideologies have shaped the course of human society. His arguments are sharp, often challenging the assumptions we take for granted about progress, morality, and what it means to be human.

One of the most powerful aspects of the book is how it connects ancient history to today’s world. Harari shows that the way we live — from our economic systems to our social structures — is built on layers of historical decisions and chance events. Understanding this history feels essential to making sense of the challenges we face today, from technology and politics to ethics and the future of our species.

Harari’s writing is clear, witty, and sometimes provocative, which makes reading the book an intellectual adventure. It’s not just a history book; it’s a mirror that forces you to question your own values and assumptions about the world.

> “You could never convince a monkey to give you a banana by promising him limitless bananas after death in monkey heaven.”
> 
> 
> *Sapiens*
> 

*Sapiens* is more than just a recounting of our past — it is a meditation on the forces that made us who we are and a call to think critically about the future we are building. It left me inspired, unsettled, and eager to keep questioning everything I thought I knew about humanity.

## 9.  Shards of Earth Series by Adrian Tchaikovsky

Adrian Tchaikovsky’s *Shards of Earth* series pulled me into one of the most imaginative space operas I’ve read in years. What makes it so special is the way Tchaikovsky creates a galaxy teeming with bizarre, fascinating alien species and cultures, each with its own distinct biology, psychology, and history. The universe feels truly alien, not just in appearance but in thought — something that is surprisingly rare in science fiction.

I loved the dynamics within the crew of the *Vulture God*. Each member feels vividly alive, with their own quirks, flaws, and loyalties, making the book as much about relationships and chosen family as it is about cosmic threats. The protagonist, Idris, is one of the most compelling characters I’ve encountered: physically frail and emotionally scarred, but possessing a mind that can literally touch the incomprehensible. His quiet strength and strange gift turn him into an unlikely but unforgettable hero.

The mysterious Architects — the massive, unknowable entities that reshape planets into eerie works of art — were a highlight for me. The way they appear, destroy entire worlds for no discernible reason, and vanish again is terrifying and awe-inspiring at the same time. Tchaikovsky manages to capture the feeling of confronting something truly beyond human comprehension.

What I loved most about the series is how it blends big ideas with personal stakes. There’s a perfect balance between galaxy-shaping mysteries, tense space battles, and intimate moments of connection between the characters. The books kept me constantly wondering what would happen next and left me with a sense of cosmic wonder long after I finished.

> “Some things are too vast to be fought. The best you can do is survive long enough to learn their shape.”
> 
> 
> *Shards of Earth*
> 

The *Shards of Earth* series is more than just space adventure — it’s a story about survival, identity, and what it means to find hope when faced with an indifferent universe. It made me feel the immensity of space while also caring deeply about the small group of people trying to navigate it together.

## 10. A Crack in Creation by J. Duodna & Samuel Sternberg

Jennifer Doudna and Samuel Sternberg's *A Crack in Creation* holds a special place in my heart as it was one of the defining books I read during my Masters in Biomedicine. What makes this book extraordinary is how it combines cutting-edge science with the very human story behind one of the most significant discoveries of our time.

I was truly amazed by the dedication and scientific imagination displayed by Doudna and her colleague Emmanuelle Charpentier. Their journey to understanding and developing CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technology wasn't just about brilliant science—it was about persistence, collaboration, and seeing possibilities where others didn't. The book beautifully captures both the eureka moments and the countless hours of meticulous work that led to their breakthrough.

What inspired me most, and ultimately influenced my decision to pursue a career in research, was the genuine passion for creating knowledge that benefits humanity. Doudna and Sternberg don't shy away from the ethical complexities of gene editing technology, instead tackling them head-on with nuance and responsibility. This honest approach to discussing both the promise and potential perils of CRISPR technology showed me what it truly means to be a conscientious scientist.

The book also highlights something I've come to value deeply in my own work: the power of scientific collaboration. The international partnerships, the sharing of ideas across disciplines, and the collegiality that propelled CRISPR research forward demonstrated how science at its best transcends borders and competition. These collaborative relationships weren't just professionally productive—they fostered genuine friendships that sustained the researchers through challenges and setbacks.

> **"CRISPR had snuck up on me—I'd never explicitly set out to study it—but its growing impact on my life had been impossible to miss."***A Crack in Creation*
> 

Perhaps what impressed me most about the book was how effectively it explains complex genetic concepts to non-specialists. The authors use clear analogies and brilliant visual representations to make CRISPR-Cas9 understandable without oversimplifying. The illustrations depicting how this molecular machinery works were particularly helpful—I still find myself thinking in terms of these visual models when explaining gene editing to others.

Reading *A Crack in Creation* during my studies didn't just teach me about a revolutionary technology; it showed me what kind of researcher I wanted to become—one who combines scientific rigor with ethical consideration and collaborative spirit. Years later, I still recommend this book to anyone interested in understanding not just how CRISPR works, but how transformative scientific discoveries actually happen.
