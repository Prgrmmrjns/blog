Have you ever wondered why the cells in your body can be so different? A muscle cell is entirely different than a neuron or a skin cell. If you read my previous post about stem cells you might know why. It is not because the cells have a different DNA, rather it is because particular genes are turned off in different cells. For example, a fat cell does not need to produce filamentous proteins such as a muscle cell or a skin cell does not produce neurotransmitters such as a neuron. So how are genes turned on and off in our genome? Well, the answer is Epigenetics. There are various epigenetic mechanisms at work to regulate our genes. In the following I would like to introduce all major ones to you.


## 1. Histone modifications
What are histones you may ask? The name actually gives a good hint as you might imagine histones like stones. A histone is an octamer, a protein complex consisting of eight subunits. The DNA is packed around those histones similar to a thread around a stone. Together, DNA and histones are called chromatin. For a gene that is silenced in a particular cell the DNA is tightly packed around the histone. This makes it hard for proteins implicated in gene regulation to attach to the DNA. In contrast, genes that are active are loosely packed or do not have a histone nearby which allows binding of proteins. Histone modifications are the way by which a cell creates and maintains an open chromatin state or a closed chromatin state. Enzymes can attach certain chemical groups such as a methyl group or a acetyl group which can impact the compactibility of the chromatin or lead to the recruitment of other proteins. Some important histone modifications are H3K4me3 or H3K27me3. If these names sound strange to you don't worry. The first part tells you which histone protein is modified, e.g. the histone protein H3. Next, the name tells you which amino acid is affected, e.g. K3 means the third lysine going from the C-terminal. And finally the last part tells you the type of histone modification. me3 for exampples states that three methylation groups are present.
